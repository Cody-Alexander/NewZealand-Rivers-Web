About the Whanganui River

The Whanganui River, known as Te Awa Tupua, is more than just a waterway, It is the country's third-longest river, and has special status reflecting the deep cultural and spiritual connection the Māori have with this majestic river.

Beauty

The Whanganui River is celebrated for its serene beauty and lush, green surroundings. The river’s winding course through dense forests and valleys creates a peaceful environment. Canoe journeys along the river offer visitors a chance to immerse themselves in the tranquil beauty of this historic waterway.

Culture

For centuries, the Whanganui River has been central to the lives of the Whanganui iwi. The river is considered taonga, reflecting the deep spiritual connection. In 2017, the Whanganui River became the first in the world to be gifted special status granting it rights and protections under the law.

History

The Whanganui River has a long history as a vital trade route and transportation link for both Māori and European settlers. During the 19th century, steamboats were a common sight as they carried goods and passengers along the river. Today, the river is an important symbol of New Zealand’s legal innovation and its commitment to honoring rights and traditions.
